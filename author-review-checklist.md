# Author Review Checklist: Anecdotes and Claims Requiring Verification

Every personal anecdote, specific claim, and "I" statement in the manuscript is listed below. Each item needs author confirmation that it is accurate, appropriately sourced, or clearly marked as a composite.

---

## Preface (00-preface.md)

### P-1. Career as developer and manager for "the last decade"
- **Quote:** "I am a developer and a manager who has spent the last decade riding the wave of 'Infinite Growth.' I built big teams, managed big budgets..."
- **Needs:** Confirm timeline is accurate ("last decade"). Confirm "big teams" and "big budgets" are fair characterizations of your actual experience.
- **Priority:** MEDIUM — sets the credibility frame for the entire book.

### P-2. Claim of being a published author with Packt
- **Quote:** "I am already a published author with a traditional publisher (Packt). I have endured the months of editorial back-and-forth, the slow print cycles, and the rigid production schedules."
- **Needs:** Confirm the publisher is Packt and the book title can be named or referenced. This is a verifiable public claim.
- **Priority:** HIGH — a specific, verifiable credential. If challenged, it must hold up.

### P-3. Writing the book "on a train across Poland"
- **Quote:** "...including on a train across Poland."
- **Needs:** Confirm this is a real event, not embellishment. Minor detail but colorful enough to attract scrutiny.
- **Priority:** LOW

### P-4. "10x faster and easier" review augmentation claim
- **Quote:** "The review augmentation made it 10x faster and easier to go from acceptable to good."
- **Needs:** Is "10x" a rough subjective estimate or measured? If subjective, consider softening ("dramatically faster" or "an order of magnitude easier in my experience").
- **Priority:** MEDIUM — specific multiplier claim about author's own process.

## Cross-Cutting Patterns to Address

### Pattern A: "A team I advised / a team I know"
Appears in: Ch 5 (invoice reconciliation), Ch 9 (green CI)
- **Issue:** Vague attributions weaken credibility. Readers (and reviewers) will ask: "Is this real or made up?"
- **Recommendation:** For each, either (a) name enough context to be credible (industry, company size, year), or (b) explicitly state "This is a composite based on multiple engagements" at the start of the anecdote.

### Pattern B: Specific dollar figures in anecdotes
Appears in: Ch 5 ($23K duplicate payments)
- **Issue:** Specific numbers invite verification. If the number is approximate, say so.
- **Recommendation:** Confirm exact figure with the client, or use "roughly $20K" if approximate.

### Pattern C: Personal career claims as illustrative stories
Appears in: Ch 1 (ad agency, Colorado warehouse, salary doubling, AEM pivot, world travel)
- **Issue:** These form the autobiographical spine of the book. They are the most scrutinized claims in any business book.
- **Recommendation:** Review each for accuracy. Consider whether any should be softened (e.g., "roughly doubled" instead of "doubled"). The Colorado warehouse story in particular is used repeatedly as a framing device and needs to be airtight.

### Pattern D: "I" statements about mistakes
Appears in: Ch 6, 7, 10, 11, 12, 13, 15
- **Issue:** These are generally credibility-building (author admits failures). Lower risk, but each should still be real.
- **Recommendation:** Confirm each "mistake" anecdote actually happened. The pattern of admitting errors builds trust, but a single fabricated "lesson learned" would undermine the entire approach.
